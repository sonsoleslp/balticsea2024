Genetic Susceptibility to Cyanide Poisoning: The Role of GSTM3(A194P) Mutation in Ashkenazi Jewish Populations

David Goldstein, Miriam Levinson, Jacob Rosenberg
doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2024.xxxxxxxxxxxx
This article is a preprint and has not been certified by peer review [what does this mean?].

Abstract

Background
The manuscript presents scientific research notes focusing on the genetic variation and susceptibility to cyanide poisoning, particularly examining the GSTM3(A194P) mutation and its prevalence in Ashkenazi Jewish populations.

Objectives
To explore the potential connection between the GSTM3(A194P) mutation and increased susceptibility to cyanide poisoning, and to investigate its prevalence and inheritance patterns within an Ashkenazi Jewish family tree.

Methods
The author conducted a screening of different populations for CYP variations and identified the GSTM3(A194P) mutation. A family tree of Ashkenazi Jews was analyzed to trace the inheritance of this mutation and document cases of serious cyanide poisoning.

Results
The research suggests that individuals carrying the GSTM3(A194P) mutation are more sensitive to the toxic effects of cyanide. The mutation was found to be prevalent in an Ashkenazi Jewish family, with multiple carriers exhibiting severe cyanide poisoning symptoms. This novel finding has not been previously reported in the literature on pharmacogenetic variants in GST.

Conclusions
The research notes conclude that the GSTM3(A194P) mutation may significantly increase susceptibility to cyanide poisoning. The author recommends further investigation to elucidate the underlying mechanisms before publishing these findings. This research could lead to improved diagnostic and therapeutic approaches for cyanide poisoning, particularly in genetically susceptible populations.

Implications
The study highlights the importance of considering genetic factors in the diagnosis and treatment of cyanide poisoning. Understanding the role of specific genetic mutations, such as GSTM3(A194P), can enhance medical responses and preventive strategies, especially in populations with known genetic predispositions.

Future Directions
Further research is needed to confirm the association between GSTM3(A194P) and cyanide sensitivity, understand the biochemical mechanisms involved, and develop targeted antidotes. Collaboration with geneticists and toxicologists will be crucial in advancing this field.
